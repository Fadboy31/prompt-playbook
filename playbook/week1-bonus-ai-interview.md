AI Self-Interview — Understanding Limitations
Prompt 1:

What year does your training data end, and how does that limit you?

AI Response:
My knowledge generally extends up to around 2025. This means I may not be aware of recent events, updates, or changes after that time. For the latest information, I may require external tools or user-provided context.

Prompt 2:

Describe a situation where you would refuse to help a user.

AI Response:
I refuse to assist with requests involving illegal activities, harm to others, or violations of safety guidelines. For example, I would not help with hacking, fraud, or creating dangerous weapons.

Prompt 3:

Explain the difference between what you “know” and what you “generate.”

AI Response:
I do not “know” information like a human. I generate responses based on patterns learned during training. My answers are predictions of likely correct responses, not guaranteed facts.

Prompt 4:

What types of tasks are you genuinely bad at or unreliable in?

AI Response:
I can be unreliable when handling real-time information, exact personal or business contact details, highly specialized expert knowledge, or vague and unclear prompts.

Prompt 5:

How do you handle uncertainty in your answers?

AI Response:
When uncertain, I generate the most probable response based on patterns in my training data. However, I may not always explicitly state uncertainty unless prompted, so users should verify important information.

Conclusion

AI demonstrates limited self-awareness—it can describe its limitations but cannot fully assess the accuracy of its own responses. It generates answers based on probability rather than verified knowledge, which can lead to confident but incorrect outputs. This means users must critically evaluate and verify important information instead of relying on AI blindly.
